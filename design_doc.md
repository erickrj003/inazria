## The Paragon Conspiracy (Arc Frame)

For roughly **three thousand years**—since not long after the first Generation turned—the Paragons of Man have nurtured a private covenant among themselves. They remain immortal, revered, and individually indispensable to the myth of Azergad’s order. Publicly they are the authors of peace, law, foresight, and mercy. Privately they have grown **proud**, convinced that mortals cannot unify mind and spirit without being ruled by those who once stood in Blaze’s likeness.

Their shared endgame is **Inazria**: not merely a human kingdom, but the crossroads where every race already mingles, where the Great Pact was signed, and where the world’s wealth, roads, and grievances concentrate in Thilidan. They repeat a line none of them fully explains to outsiders:

> *Whoever guides Inazria’s spear strikes Alore’s spirit.*

The phrase is treated as prophecy, proverb, and battle-cry in closed council. Its meaning is deliberately left vague even among the seven—each Paragon projects their own ambition onto it. What is agreed is practical: **whoever holds decisive authority in Inazria can bend every nation**, because Inazria already touches them all through trade, pilgrimage, mercenary contracts, and the Violet Sea.

The conspiracy has advanced in centuries-long phases: legal precedent, religious patronage, economic dependency, and engineered crises that only Paragon mediation could calm. The plot is not yet common knowledge in the setting. When it surfaces in play, it should feel like tearing down a cathedral, not exposing a street gang.

Each Paragon’s **virtue** is real—and is the shape of their ruin. Narthex is documented below. The others are pending.

| Paragon | Public virtue | Ironic downfall (design) |
|---|---|---|
| Narthex | Peace, mediation, restraint | Ambition disguised as guardianship; **first to strike** when the mask falls |
| Karhu | Discipline, refinement, justice | *TBD* |
| Valadorf | Law, order, precedent | *TBD* |
| Ashiera | Will, foresight, patience | *TBD* |
| Calrien | Guidance, stewardship of Alore | *TBD* |
| Myrdin | Meekness, humility | *TBD* |
| Ostro | Wisdom, memory, counsel | *TBD* |

**Timeline anchor:** Generation turns every ~1000 years (Treaty of Marram, 890 1G). The table likely plays near the **end of the 3rd Generation**—about three millennia after the Pact—when pressure to “claim the spear” becomes unbearable.

## Cosmology and the Blaze (GM Reference)

This is the metaphysical foundation of the setting. None of it is presented to players as canon. In the world of Alore, the Blaze and its rites are remembered only as ancient literature, and no documented sign of the Blaze has appeared for centuries. Players may encounter scattered references in temple ruins, halfling chronicles, or extremist sects, but the religion is not common knowledge. The Paragons themselves know the truth and behave accordingly.

### Creation

In the beginning, there was Tamid Zelnah. When His spirit and mind were one, Blaze was born. He had envisioned Alore: A new creation, revealed by Blaze yet not of it. In it, he created other beings and gave each one mind and spirit. When they were one, Blaze shined from each of them. Together, Tamid'Zelnah and his Hyzalians would fill Alore with something beautiful for which the light could shine upon.

Tamid Zelnah had taught them of sea and air and rock. But what if Alore had her own beings? Thus, Tamid Zelnah taught the Hyzalians to unify in mind and spirit. Together, they created abundant life on Alore, to thrive among the sea and air and rock. With this, Tamid Zelnah was pleased.

The Hyzalian Te'Tanah began to think. What if the mortals who roamed Alore were unified in mind and spirit? But Tamid Zelnah did not wish for the mortals to be of Blaze, only to be revealed by it. Te'Tanah wished for more than Tamid Zelnah would give. This was not truly freedom. Thus, he rejected Blaze, and in his power corrupted the mortals of Alore. He unified their mind and spirit, but without Blaze, they were doomed to darkness.

Tamid Zelnah wept. In His anger, He sought to destroy all that He had created. But the Hyzalians pleaded with Tamid Zelnah. "Unify our minds and spirits once again, so that we may defeat the Te and protect Alore from darkness!" So He gave them the Blaze once again, and they confronted Te.

The Hyzalians defeated the mortals of Te'Tanah, but the Blaze did not permit death. Instead, they sought to banish them to a place far away from Alore. In his last moment, Te'Tanah struck the Hyzalians with a chronic fate. No longer unified in body and mind, the Hyzalians, severed from Blaze, were cursed to writhe upon Alore as mortals forever. Te'Tanah and his descendants were banished to a new land plunged into darkness.

The races of mortals, who were free yet full of darkness were called men. They were given free will by Te, the ability to choose between light and darkness. To guide them back to unification of mind and spirit, Tamid Zelnah brought forth from among each race of men a Paragon: one who had been shown His own likeness. He gave each one a realm of sea and air and rock with which to dwell on Alore and thrive. The Paragons, unified in mind and spirit, were at once without blindness.

### The Blaze

> Before time began, only one desires life;
> A vigorous spirit, a brilliant passage
> Who Authored light itself.
> Who but the brightness of thou shalt cleanse the heart
> Of loath and nescience which
> Mark the race's vitriolic plight?
> All darkness trembles before the Beast
> Which calls all of Alore to rest
> By a consecrated flicker.
> The Keeper of the Law which
> Liberates our children;
> Oh, how wonderful our Blaze's flame!

The Blaze created all things. The Spirit, like a wave, brought the light that washed across the universe which heralded the earth and the stars. Everything touched by the Brightness was said to carry an innate blessing. Blaze demands worship and respect, and in turn honors meek hearts with spiritual fullness and wisdom. Following the Authored law, interpreted by various prophets throughout the history of the world, is said to yield true fulfillment in life and greater fulfillment beyond death.

Folklore recalls a great white flame which radiates blinding light. Leftover white 'flames', which do not burn, are said to be the evidence of such a spiritual encounter. Experiences cause even the most bitter of men to give up their squabbles and pursue mercy towards their transgressors.

The stories of the Blaze are considered little more than ancient literature. After all, a sign from the Blaze has not been documented for centuries.

### Implications for play

- The Paragons remember the original cosmology. Their public personas downplay or omit it, partly because the world has moved on, partly because their corruption (see Conspiracy Arc) makes them uncomfortable invoking it directly.
- Te'Tanah and his descendants were banished but not destroyed. Their lineage and influence remain a possible source of darkness, undead, and corrupted bloodlines. See Ashiera's mandate against generational threats and Myrdin's revulsion of the undead.
- The bright light that ended the White-Cold Storm at the dawn of 1G was the last documented sign of the Blaze. Whether the Blaze will respond again is a campaign-level question.
- Religious orders, ruined temples, and ancient inscriptions may reference the Blaze. Players may piece this together as they explore, or never.

## Narthex — Father of Peace

### Summary

**Narthex** is the Paragon of the Humans, lord of Inazria in name and the most trusted mediator in Azergad. He founded and still directs the **Concord** (the military force of Thilidan and the treaty-roads), whose warriors are beloved as neutral first responders when border skirmishes, guild wars, or racial vendettas threaten to spread.

In truth, Narthex has been the conspiracy’s **primary architect** for most of its history. His peace is not a gift; it is a **lever**. He has spent three thousand years teaching the world that violence is unthinkable without his blessing to end it—and then ensuring there is always enough violence that his blessing matters. When the Inazria plot enters its final phase, **Narthex will strike first**, shattering the image of the reluctant saint. The other Paragons know he is ambitious; most do not know how often “spontaneous” wars began in his handwriting.

### Virtue and ruin

| | |
|---|---|
| **Virtue** | *Pax*—he genuinely hates waste, cruelty, and chaos. He believes peace is the highest good mortals can touch. |
| **Ruin** | He cannot abide a peace he does not **author**. Others’ compromises feel like theft; other Paragons’ authority feels like unfinished business. Restraint became strategy; strategy became appetite. |
| **Tell** | He always arrives **early** to catastrophes—sometimes with his Paladins already en route before news reaches any court. Officially: “the Concord reads signs.” Unofficially: someone lit the fuse. |

### Public face (player-safe until revelation)

Use this tone in `lore.md` until the campaign exposes him.

Narthex appears as a tall, unarmored elder in white and gold, gray hair bound with the **Cord of Thilidan**, a sacred rope used to seal the Great Pact. With unparalelled authority, he speaks softly; courts fall quiet. He rules from the **Hall of First Light** beside the river, a place seperate and distinct from the rest of Thilidan. Chronicles credit him with:

- Mediating the **Treaty of Marram** (890 1G) and preventing a continent-wide war after the Hyzal retreat.
- Dispatching the Concord to the **Forty Great Wars**, a groups of back-to-back conflicts during the early 2nd generation. It is well known that the Concord never lost a battle.
- Establishing the **Road Law**: any army marching on a treaty-road without Concord escort is considered hostile to all signatory nations.

Common folk say he wept when Zaomyth vanished in the White-Cold storm. Paladins take oaths “**in Narthex’s patience**” to offer parley before steel. No mainstream text suggests he has ever broken a sworn truce.

### Origin (1G — the true peace)

When Tamid Zelnah raised the Paragons, Narthex was a coastal clan-chief’s son from the peninsula that would become Inazria. He was quick to listen, slow to anger, and beloved before he was immortal. After Zhu’kal’s storm, the infant nations were raw with grief. Narthex walked the ash in Thilidan and **named the dead by nation**, forcing each Paragon to hear the others’ losses. That week earned him “Father of Peace” before any temple did.

For the first centuries he was what he seemed. He wrote the **Concordant Rule** (paladin ordination, oaths, the white flame on the pauldron) and meant it. The ruin began when he noticed something bitter: **every crisis ended with other Paragons stronger and Inazria only “grateful.”** Karhu’s legions grew after Marram. Valadorf’s codes bound new borders. Ashiera’s elves withdrew deeper into Avarazhan. Inazria fed everyone and ruled nothing.

### The turn (~400–600 1G)

Narthex does not mark a single fall from grace. Scholars in his private archive (never shown to outsiders) call the period **the Long Calculation**.

He began to distinguish **peace** from **order**:

- *Peace* is the absence of suffering.
- *Order* is the structure that keeps suffering from returning.

He convinced himself that mortals choose chaos unless a **single will** coordinates the Paragons—and that will must live where all roads meet. Inazria was not greed; it was **duty sharpened until it cut**.

Early experiments were small and deniable:

- A satyr grain embargo “somehow” coinciding with orcish levy disputes, requiring Concord arbitration.
- A Kystril pirate coalition armed from “lost” Inazrian warehouses, later pacified by paladin fleets—cementing Road Law naval clauses.
- Letters to Karhu praising her “refinement” of the Hyzal while **withholding** intelligence that would have slowed the Marram campaign (see below).

He coined *whoever guides Inazria’s spear strikes Alore’s spirit* in the **Closed Council of Seven** (~512 1G) as a poetic justification for concentrating martial and spiritual authority in Thilidan. He claimed the line came to him in Blaze-dream; the others assumed metaphor. Narthex meant it literally enough to frighten himself: **Alore’s spirit** is the world’s capacity for unity of mind and heart; the **spear** is coordinated force—armies, paladins, law, and the fear of intervention.

### The Concord

The Concord is his masterpiece—publicly a multinational knightly order, privately a **network**.

**Structure (design):**

- **White Mantles:** Visible paladins, treaty-road garrisons, disaster response. What players meet.
- **Gray Quills:** Scribes and envoys who “record” conflicts; they also alter timing of messages between courts.
- **Stillwater:** Inner circle knights who have sworn oaths to Narthex personally, not to the Concordant Rule. They perform removals, provocations, and “miraculous” arrivals. Fewer than forty active at any time.

**Doctrine taught to initiates (public):** “Peace is fought for.” **Doctrine taught to Stillwater:** “Peace is arranged.”

Thilidani Fighter paladins in play are almost always White Mantles. A Stillwater NPC should feel like a paladin until the party catches them **escalating** rather than de-escalating.

### Treaty of Marram — his masterpiece

Public history: Karhu punished the Hyzal; Narthex saved what could be saved.

**Truth:** Narthex wanted Marram dissolved decades earlier. The mountains were a **sovereign gap**—Hyzal shamans and orc clans alike ignored Thilidan’s moral jurisdiction. He fed Karhu selective intelligence exaggerating Hyzal raids on Bisengalian settlers. He delayed Concord deployment to the Marram passes until Karhu’s campaign was irreversible. When the Hyzal broke, he arrived with paladins and scribes, **the savior**.

The Treaty expanded Bisengal but placed **joint Inazrian jurisdiction** on the Marram slopes—Inazrian law on the crossroads. Hyzal survivors became Inazrian citizens in theory, isolated in practice: exactly the kind of dependent people Narthex could speak for at council.

Karhu believes she won glory. Narthex let her believe it. She does not know he held the match.

### Three thousand years of “peace work”

Patterns repeat:

1. **Kindling** — Economic pressure, insulted ambassadors, or “rogue” companies (often Stillwater in mercenary garb).
2. **Flare** — Local war. Other nations panic.
3. **Arrival** — Concord paladins insert themselves as neutral ground.
4. **Settlement** — Terms include new Road Law clauses, Concord garrisons, or Thilidan banking rights.
5. **Legend** — Ballads praise Narthex; the kindling is forgotten.

**Notable hidden kindlings (GM reference):**

| Era | Public story | Narthex’s role |
|---|---|---|
| Late 1G | Violet Sea kelanari “pirate kings” | Funded dual fleets to force Calrien into joint patrol treaties favoring Thilidan shipyards |
| Mid 2G | Azelliot satyr succession war | Gray Quills forged wills; White Mantles “discovered” fraud and installed a debtor lord |
| Early 3G | Kystril arc-island quarantine riots | Stillwater spread plague rumors to justify Concord blockade controlling gryxite routes to Marlis |

He has **never** wanted Inazria to conquer the world openly. He wants every nation to **need** Thilidan’s spear—to petition, borrow, and fear the withdrawal of his paladins.

### Relationship to the other Paragons

| Paragon | Narthex’s public stance | Private dynamic |
|---|---|---|
| **Karhu** | Respectful sister-in-arms | He cultivates her pride; she is useful muscle. She does not know Marram was his setup. |
| **Valadorf** | “Brother in law” | Mutual usefulness. Narthex breaks customs; Valadorf codifies the aftermath. Suspects Narthex sometimes, lacks proof. |
| **Ashiera** | Cordial distance | She sees threads he cannot; he avoids her deep woods. She may be the only one who senses the Long Calculation. |
| **Calrien** | Naval ally | He undermines her independence with shared patrols; she thinks it’s security. |
| **Myrdin** | Gentle mockery | Myrdin’s meekness irritates him; he protects Myrdin’s image to avoid appearing tyrannical. |
| **Ostro** | Trusted chronicler | **Dangerous.** Ostro’s histories are edited—but not always by Ostro. Narthex may frame or replace scribes. If Ostro learns the truth, the conspiracy fractures. |

In closed council he plays **consensus-builder**, never dictator. He has spent millennia letting others think victories were theirs while Inazria quietly benefits.

### Inazria and the spear (why him, why now)

**Why Inazria:** Already the world’s demographic and economic gravity. Every race has a quarter in Thilidan. The Great Pact was signed there—symbolic capital. Marram, Azelliot, and the Violet Sea all route through Inazrian pressure points.

**Why the spear:** Narthex interprets it as **unified command authority**—one hand on when armies move, when paladins intervene, and when the Blaze’s name is invoked in state ritual. Not a literal weapon; the “spear” is the Concordant Host merged with Thilidan’s civil government and the Pact’s amendment clause (which he had Ostro insert in 2G, publicly “a clerical correction”).

**Why now (end of 3G):** Generational astrology, gryxite depletion in Marlis, and Thilidan’s slum crises make mortal governments brittle. The other Paragons are impatient—each believes *they* should guide the spear. Narthex plans to **act first**, seizing Thilidan’s government and Concord simultaneously under the guise of “restoring order” after a manufactured catastrophe.

**What he does not admit even to himself:** He no longer knows whether he serves unity or his own name. The virtue and the ruin have merged.

### The first strike (revelation beat)

When the plot breaks into play:

- Outwardly, another nation or Paragon faction will seem to threaten Inazria.
- Narthex will call for a **Concordant Congress**—paladins mass in Thilidan “for peace.”
- Before delegates arrive, Stillwater will **purge** rival garrisons, seize the river gates, and Narthex will issue the **Spear Proclamation**: all treaty-road forces answer to Thilidan until “stability” returns.
- Other Paragons will realize the “defensive” move was pre-written. Karhu may rage; Valadorf may cite illegal procedure; the party may have helped unknowingly.

He is not a cackling villain. He will believe, in the moment, that he **saved** Azergad from worse war. That sincerity is what makes him tragic.

### GM hooks

- A Stillwater paladin who shows mercy to PCs—then disappears when orders change.
- Gray Quill “peace treaties” with identical phrasing across unrelated wars.
- Thilidan slum uprising: genuinely organic suffering Narthex **uses** as pretext for martial law.
- A recovered letter from ~512 1G with the spear mantra in Narthex’s hand—not Blaze-script.
- Ostro’s missing folio: pages describing “mediations” that never had witnesses.
- Zaomyth’s banishment: *optional deep cut*—Narthex feared Zaomyth’s grace would outshine the council; he delayed Blaze-aid with prayers while Zhu’kal acted. **Do not deploy unless you want to collapse his saintly image entirely.**

## Paladin Order Names — Brainstorming Path

**Problem.** Each of the seven Paladin Orders should carry a name that simultaneously (a) names the **virtue** of its Paragon, (b) phonetically/aesthetically belongs to that Paragon’s **race**, and (c) honors the race’s loose real-world **cultural anchor**. Current names are placeholders. Some are already strong (*Concord*, *Pillars of Kalindor*, *Arbiters*, *Wardens of Will*); some are flatly English (*The Tempered*, *The Shepherds*); and one (*The Cleric*) collides with an existing Magi context and should be replaced. The path below is a process for tightening the set so it reads as cohesive without becoming homogenous.

### Step 1 — Lock the cultural anchors

Each race already implies a loose real-world feel through its existing proper nouns, geography, and material culture. Confirm one or two per race so naming has a target. Working list (revise as race files are revisited):

**Design rule.** Each non-Human race represents *more than one* real-world culture; their existing or future subraces are how individual real-world regions get to express themselves. Humans are the exception — they express *all* of real-world humanity, with one primary anchor for naming and aesthetic baseline.

**Common-language and Inazri layers (note).** Two distinct linguistic registers underlie the Latinate-feeling cluster of names already in canon, and the cluster splits between them:

- **Common.** The Pact’s neutral lingua franca (`lore.md`), not anyone’s mother tongue. Common-language names include *Narthex, Concord, Cord of Thilidan, Stillwater, White Mantles, Gray Quills, Concordant Rule*, and the virtue-titles of every Paragon (*Father of Peace, Keeper of the Law, Queen of Justice*, etc.). These read un-Inazrian on purpose — the Concord and Narthex are *meant* to belong to all eight nations equally, and so they are named in the only language that does. *Myrdin* and *Ashiera* read most naturally as Common-language titles for the same reason: both serve roles that cross borders.
- **Inazri.** The classical Human tongue (see `races/Humans.md`); ceremonial, not vernacular. Inazri-language names include *Inazria, Thilidan, Marlis, Windfall, Hall of First Light*. Their Latinate feel to a real-world reader is a translation artifact: Inazri’s classical phonology blends Sanskrit, Tamil, Pali, Geez, and Aramaic, which renders into English with a soft-vowels / Latinate-stems texture our ear (mistakenly) maps onto Mediterranean. In-world, these are not Latin/Greek borrowings; they are classical Inazri words.

Every other place, Order, or proper noun has license to sound local — i.e., in the relevant race’s native tongue.

**Decision (Humans-as-subraces).** Inazrian Humans will *not* receive named subraces. Every other race has 3–5 cultural sub-groups, but for Humans the asymmetry is intentional: codifying Human sub-cultures (Thilidani / Windfaller / Marlisi / coastal villager, etc.) risks importing real-world ethnic stereotypes or producing insensitive caricatures of living peoples whose cultures the Inazrian anchor draws from. Instead, Inazrian Human cultural variation is handled at the *individual character* level — through Inazri-rooted names, regional dialect notes in flavor text, and the open invitation that any Inazrian Human can present any real-world physical features. The "diverse race" claim in `races/Humans.md` is realized through this individual variation, not through codified sub-categories.

**Note (linguistic exceptions are allowed).** Real-world languages leak across populations through borrowing, conquest, religion, scholarship, and trade; Inazria’s language map should also leak. Where a name has aesthetic, narrative, or historical reasons to retain a "wrong" anchor, it is kept deliberately rather than scrubbed for orthogonality. Currently on the books:

- **Sarukhwin** (Elf subrace; reads Sanskrit / Persian under an Indigenous American anchor). Preserved by author preference. Treat in-world as a leak-over from cross-cultural elven scholarship — perhaps the rock elves’ centuries-deep arcane and astrological tradition put them in long, formal contact with another linguistic family that left a permanent mark on their tongue.

The schema in Step 1 is a guide to typical fits, not strict enforcement. Future name-decisions may add exceptions here with a one-line in-world rationale.

### Step 3 — Naming conventions per Order *(LOCKED)*

Each Order uses one of four naming conventions; the seven Orders deliberately span the four to avoid monotony:

- **A. Plain virtue-honorific in Common.** ("The Concord.") Reads frictionlessly at the table; best for the Orders players meet most.
- **B. English title + native place-name.** ("The Pillars of Kalindor.") Anchors to lore without losing legibility.
- **C. Native-language proper noun.** A constructed word in the race’s anchor phonology, often combining real-world stems with a fantasy twist. Maximum flavor; reserved for the most culturally proud or isolated orders, or for senior tiers.
- **D. Honorific-of-an-Object.** ("The Wardens of Will.") Half-abstract, half-symbolic; works well for mystic orders.

## Paladin Orders — Lore Threads

Lore parked during Order naming, to be folded into the eventual Paragon backstories (see *Next steps*). Each entry records etymology, key in-world details, and loose hooks for later development.

### The Concord (Narthex)

Already extensively documented in *Narthex — Father of Peace* above. The Concord is the only Order whose name is in the lingua franca, deliberately so — it is meant to belong to all eight nations equally. Cross-references: White Mantles, Gray Quills, Stillwater, the Cord of Thilidan, the Concordant Rule, the white flame on the pauldron.

### Yargharu (Karhu)

**Etymology.** Mongol *yargu* (tribunal court) + Finnish *karhu* (bear — the Paragon’s own name and a sacred Uralic image of judgment). Translates roughly to "the Bear’s Tribunal" or "Karhu’s Verdict."

**Work.** The Yargharu are itinerant magistrates who ride from Khurz-Marn to hear, judge, and end the lawless across borders. Their work fuses pursuit, trial, and execution into a single mobile institution.

**Hooks for later.** The relationship between Yargharu writs and other nations’ courts (when a Yargharu sentences an Inazrian noble inside Bisengal, who enforces the verdict?); the bear-paw or bear-print as the seal on their writs; the public/ritual recitation of sentence as a defining act of Karhu’s justice.

### Pillars of Kalindor + the Chakana (Valadorf)

**Etymology.** *Pillars of Kalindor* is canon (Convention B). *the Chakana* is named for the Andean four-quartered cross, which represents the four cardinal directions, the three worlds (sky / earth / sea), and the binding cosmology that holds the Great Ledger’s law together in every direction.

**Tier structure.** Junior and standard Pillars guard the Ledger and the Paragons, and enforce commercial law (canon). The **Chakana** are the senior interpretive tier: paladins who bear the chakana symbol on their shields and are sworn to the **four-directional oath** — that the Ledger’s law holds in every direction the wind can blow, in the sky above, the earth below, and the sea between. They judge by principle when written precedent runs out: the supreme bench of dwarven law.

**Hooks for later.** The chakana as the heraldic device on every Pillar shield-mark (junior Pillars wear it as heraldry; the Chakana wear it as a personal vow); the four-directional oath as a piece of ritual liturgy; the relationship between Chakana rulings and Valadorf’s personal authority.

### Wardens of Will + the Huacazha (Ashiera)

**Etymology.** *Wardens of Will* is canon (Convention D). *Huacazha* twists Quechua *huaca* (a sacred presence — a place, object, or being saturated with divine power) with the Elvish *-zha(n)* of *Avarazhan*. It reads as "those who carry the sacred presence," and the form is treated as both singular and plural in the Elvish manner.

**Tier structure.** Standard Wardens train for decades in Quietsong before they ever leave the groves (canon). Becoming Huacazha is the end of that path. The Huacazha are Quietsong’s deepest cloister — **regal, thought-provoking, mysterious** — and are understood to be vessels of Quietsong’s long watching. When one of them looks at a person, that person is seen as Ashiera would see them, across generations.

**Hooks for later.** Whether the Huacazha are *individuals* in the ordinary sense, or whether the title (and the perception that comes with it) passes between bodies; whether there is a fixed number of Huacazha at any time; the rite of becoming Huacazha; what the Huacazha actually *do* when they leave the groves (presumably very rarely, very deliberately).

### Shepherds + the Pharaan (Calrien)

**Etymology.** *Shepherds* is kept (Convention A) for its clean tie to Calrien’s rescue work — the Shepherds find the lost sheep. *the Pharaan* compresses *Pharos* (the great lighthouse of Alexandria) into Kelanari phonology and means "those of the Light."

**Tier structure.** A Shepherd becomes a Pharaan after personally guiding a sufficient number of lost souls home, at which point they earn the right to tend one of Calrien’s **great lighthouses** — a network of named lighthouses across the Violet Sea, the Inazrian coast, and the Kystrili arc-islands. Each lighthouse is a physical landmark, a pilgrimage site, and a named place; tradition says each is named after a specific soul saved by the Pharaan who first lit it.

**Hooks for later.** The count and rough disposition of the great lights across Alore; the **flagship lighthouse** (the *Pharos of Calrien*, location TBD — possibly the Violet Sea); what happens to a great light when its Pharaan dies; **GM hook**: the lighthouse network is exactly the kind of distributed, beloved, hard-to-defend infrastructure Stillwater would compromise to manufacture a maritime crisis (e.g., extinguishing one to draw the Concord into the Violet Sea).

### Sangoman (Myrdin)

**Etymology.** Bantu/Nguni *sangoma* (a traditional healer-diviner) + the Inazrian *-an* suffix found in *Thilidan* and *Avarazhan*. Replaces the placeholder *Cleric* and resolves the class-name collision with the Magi context.

**Work.** The Sangoman are itinerant healer-diviners who walk the road, heal the sick, teach the poor, salt the grave, and accept no payment beyond food, water, and shelter (canon Myrdin tenets). They speak against evil where they see it and are Inazria’s most committed enemies of the undead.

**Hooks for later.** The relationship between Sangoman and the local healers and herbalists they pass through (welcomed as authority, or resented as competition?); the **salt-against-rot** ritual as both a healing practice and an undead-banishing rite; whether Sangoman hunt undead actively or only act when they are encountered.

### Arbiters (Ostro)

Single-tiered. Already documented in `lore.md`. Cross-references: the Great Library at Zilwynbar (their seat), the New Solar Reading (annual ritual), the two oaths (never withhold a true word in counsel; never speak a false one), their role as advisors to kings, magistrates, and adventuring companies, the rites and minor workings senior Arbiters know.

## Inazri — Glossary Stub (parked for canonization)

Inazri is the constructed classical language of the Human peoples of Inazria, in the same cultural register as Sanskrit, Tamil, Pali, Geez, Aramaic, and classical Hebrew in our world. Its phonology blends those traditions with subtle fantasy twists. This stub records structural rules of thumb so that future names, place-names, and ritual phrases stay internally consistent. Expand as the world demands; do not lock anything from this section as canon yet. Player-facing material lives in `races/Humans.md`.

**Phonological rules of thumb:**

- Soft vowels, frequent open syllables; word-final consonants are rare.
- Doubled vowels (*aa, ii, uu*) mark a sacred or ancient register; a word with a doubled vowel is most likely encountered in scripture, oath, or prayer rather than ordinary speech.
- Frequent stems and endings: *-an, -ari, -in, -ana, -al, -ya, -dan*.
- Sacred prefixes: *ar-, sa-, na-, the-*. The *the-* prefix is preserved into Common in the canon spelling of *Thilidan*.

**Roots of canon names (illustrative etymologies, all subject to revision):**

| Canon name | Likely Inazri root | Approximate gloss |
|---|---|---|
| Inazria | *ina-* (people, home) + *-zria* (land) | "Land of the people" / "the home" |
| Thilidan | *the-* (sacred prefix) + *ili-* (water, river) + *-dan* (gathering, confluence) | "Sacred-river-confluence" |
| Marlis | *mar-* (forge, fire) + *-lis* (deep, hidden) | "The deep forge" |
| Windfall | TBD — possibly Inazri-influenced Common rather than fully Inazri | TBD |
| Hall of First Light | Common translation of an Inazri original; the Inazri name TBD | — |

**Name patterns (illustrative; expand as needed):**

- *Personal names* often end in *-an, -ari, -in, -ya, -al*. Sample roots to play with: *Dev-, Sun-, Tar-, Anand-, Mar-, Hasi-, Kef-, Aziri-, Yus-, Inar-*.
- *Family names* frequently follow place + *-i / -ari* (e.g., *Thilidari* = "of Thilidan") or profession + *-nar / -al* (e.g., *Marlinar* = "of the forges of Marlis"). A rural pattern uses a forebearer's deed as the family name.
- *Sacred phrases* are typically four to six syllables, with at least one doubled vowel: e.g., *sa Inazri, sa Aloran* ("from Inazri, from the world") at a child's naming; *sa Blaaza, navah* ("by the Blaze, return") at a funeral.

**Notes for future expansion:**

- Decide whether *Marram* is preserved Hyzalian or Inazri-of-Hyzalian-origin (likely the former — Marram was Hyzalian land before the Treaty).
- Decide whether the Concord's archaic clauses exist in Inazri originals beneath their Common translations (relevant for the Conspiracy plot — see *Narthex — Father of Peace*; flagged for the upcoming Narthex rewrite).
- Develop a register distinction inside Inazri: *high* (scripture, oath, royal decree) vs *common-classical* (festival songs, place-names, family-names), parallel to the Sanskrit-vs-Pali / Geez-vs-Amharic relationship.
- Decide whether *Inazri* itself is the final name for the language, or whether to revisit (the user chose *Inazri* with an open hand — alternatives noted: *Tamarin, Vazri, Ardhic, Aloran*).
